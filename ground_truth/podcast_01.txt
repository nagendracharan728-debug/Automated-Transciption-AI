Hello and welcome to another episode of the Fluency First Podcast.
I’m here with a very special guest. Hi, Carol, who is a family friend.

Carol: Hi, hi.

So at the start of every episode, we begin with a bit of small talk.
Carol, firstly, thank you so much for doing this. It’s a pleasure. I know you are a very busy person, but I’m so grateful that we managed to schedule this in.

Carol: Me too. I’m excited and looking forward to it.

So how are you today?

Carol: I’m good, thank you. I’ve been away for a few days in Worcestershire, in the Malverns. I spent time with a friend and went to Stratford-upon-Avon to see a modern play, not Shakespeare, but it was funny and very good.

That sounds interesting. And recently you went on holiday, right?

Carol: I did, yes.

Where did you go?

Carol: I went to Slovenia for the first time, which was really interesting. It’s a tiny country compared with Britain—about the size of Wales. It’s very wooded and mountainous, and also environmentally conscious. The capital, Ljubljana, is incredibly clean with no litter.

Sounds amazing. What was the food like?

Carol: It was very cosmopolitan. Traditionally the food is quite heavy and Germanic, and I don’t eat meat, but because of international influence there are many excellent continental-style restaurants.

So now it’s time for the next topic: family and connection.
Carol, you’re a family friend and know my parents. Do you remember the early days of getting to know our family?

Carol: Yes, I do. I remember coming to one of Rachel’s birthday parties when she was very young. It was in the back garden with your wider family there. I remember Wendy’s effortless hospitality—she was always so relaxed.

My mum often said that about Wendy. So how did you get to know us originally?

Carol: I got to know Ian through training work, and he later offered me work when I wanted to leave my organisation. Wendy I met through a women’s group where we shared very personal experiences. Those relationships were incredibly meaningful.

We also spent many joint family holidays together, especially in France.

Carol: Yes, particularly Brittany and the Île de Ré. Those holidays were a lifeline for me after my marriage ended. Your family welcomed me and my son, and that sense of belonging meant everything.

You were also the celebrant at my wedding.

Carol: Yes, and I was incredibly honoured to do it. A celebrant holds the ceremony together and ensures it reflects the couple’s wishes. It was a magical day.

Let’s talk about travel. Is there a place that changed you?

Carol: Cornwall. I first went there on my honeymoon, then later during a difficult period in my life. Going back helped me reclaim positive memories and create new ones. It taught me how places can hold emotional history.

What’s one travel moment you’ll never forget?

Carol: Japan. It’s a blend of ancient and modern. I remember staying in a mountain hotel near Mount Fuji. At first I hated it because I was exhausted, but the next day, after visiting an onsen bath overlooking a Japanese garden, I never wanted to leave. It taught me how perspective can change everything.

You grew up in London. What was that like?

Carol: I grew up in southeast London, near Blackheath and Greenwich. It was spacious, green, historic, and very mixed in terms of class. I came from a working-class background but lived near very wealthy areas.

You went to a grammar school and had elocution lessons, right?

Carol: Yes. We had speech and drama lessons that trained our pronunciation. It helped me academically, but it also left me with a mixed sense of identity because I didn’t sound like my family.

Do you think accent still relates to class in the UK?

Carol: Yes, I do. Although society has changed, people still make assumptions based on how you speak. Accent is one of the fastest ways people judge background.

If you could tell your younger self one thing, what would it be?

Carol: Trust your instincts more. If something feels wrong, it probably is.

What does a good life mean to you?

Carol: Health, connection, creativity, kindness, and sharing simple pleasures with people you love—good food, conversation, and belonging.

Thank you so much for being here, Carol.

Carol: Thank you. It’s been a pleasure.
